Key Antenna Types by Category
If you are looking for a general classification, antennas are broadly grouped into these types: 

    Wire Antennas: Dipoles, monopoles, and long-wire loops.
    Aperture Antennas: Parabolic (satellite) and horn antennas.
    Array Antennas: Yagi-Uda, broadside arrays, and collinear arrays.
    Patch/Microstrip: Compact antennas often found in mobile devices
//



    Dipole Antenna: A simple, two-armed antenna (like "rabbit ears") frequently used for radio and TV broadcasting.
    Monopole Antenna: A single-armed antenna often mounted over a ground plane, used in mobile devices and whip antennas.
    Yagi-Uda Antenna: A highly directional antenna with multiple parallel elements, commonly used for television reception and long-distance, outdoor, or point-to-point communication.
    Patch (Microstrip) Antenna: A flat, low-profile antenna that is easy to fabricate on circuit boards, used in Wi-Fi, GPS, and RFID.
    Loop Antenna: A conductive loop (circle or polygon) used for AM radio reception and RFID.
    Parabolic (Dish) Antenna: A high-gain, directed antenna used for satellite communication and radar systems.
    Horn Antenna: Shaped like a horn, it is used for high-frequency, wideband applications.
    Sector Antenna: A directional antenna used for covering specific, wide, or shaped areas, commonly in cellular tower base stations.
    Helical Antenna: A coil-shaped antenna used for satellite communications and in some older mobile devices.
    Log-Periodic Antenna: An antenna designed to have impedance and radiation characteristics that are regularly repetitive (logarithmic) over a wide frequency range. 

Classification by Function

    Omnidirectional: Radiates in all directions (e.g., Dipole, Monopole, Whip).
    Directional: Focuses energy in a specific direction (e.g., Yagi, Parabolic, Patch).
    Broadband: Operates over a wide range of frequencies (e.g., Log-Periodic, Horn). 

Common Applications

    Mobile Devices/IoT: PIFA (Planar Inverted-F Antenna), Patch, Whip.
    Television/Radio: Dipole, Yagi-Uda.
    Satellite/Radar: Parabolic reflector.
    Wireless Networking: Panel, Patch, Sector.

3. Classification by Frequency
Different wavelengths require different physical sizes. Antennas are often designed for specific bands: 

    VLF/LF/MF: Typically large wire or mast antennas for long-range marine or AM radio.
    VHF/UHF: Used for FM radio, TV broadcasting, and mobile communications.
    SHF/Microwave: Compact aperture or parabolic antennas for high-speed data and satellite links.

J-pole antenna (or simply the J antenna). 
It is named for its distinctive shape, which resembles the letter "J" or a trombone slide. 
Why It Looks Like a "J"
The antenna consists of two main parts that create this shape:

    The Long Arm: A vertical radiator that is roughly one-half wavelength long.
    The Short Arm: A parallel matching section (called a "stub") that is one-quarter wavelength long.
    The Base: The two arms are connected at the bottom, forming the curved base of the "J". 

Key Features

    Common Use: It is highly popular for VHF and UHF communications, especially among amateur radio operators (Ham radio).
    No Ground Needed: Unlike many vertical antennas, it is ground-independent, meaning it doesn't require extra "radials" or a metal surface to work effectively.
    DIY Friendly: Because of its simple design, many people build them out of copper plumbing pipes or ladder line (wire). 

Variations

    Slim Jim: A variation where the long radiator is folded back on itself, creating an even slimmer profile.
    Zepp Antenna: The historical predecessor used on Zeppelin airships; it is essentially a J-pole where the radiator is pulled horizontally.
